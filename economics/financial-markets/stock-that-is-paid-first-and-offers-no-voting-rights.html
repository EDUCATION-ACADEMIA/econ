Stock that is paid first and offers no voting rights

(A) common stock

(B) ** preferred stock

(C) stock exchange

(D) Primary stock

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The main difference between preferred and common stock is that preferred stock gives no voting rights to shareholders while common stock does. Preferred shareholders have priority over a company’s income, meaning they are paid dividends before common shareholders.

Concept note-2: -Preferred typically have no voting rights, whereas common stockholders do. Preferred stockholders may have the option to convert shares to common shares but not vice versa. Preferred shares may be callable where the company can demand to repurchase them at par value.

Concept note-3: -Preferred stock generally doesn’t carry voting rights. It’s issued by a company to raise capital without jeopardizing the controlling interests of the common stockholders.

Concept note-4: -A preferred stock pays stockholders set dividend payments on a regular schedule, but does not have voting rights or as much potential for capital appreciation as common stock. Investors tend to buy shares of preferred stock for their consistent income and lower financial risk if a company faces losses.

Concept note-5: -Convertible preferred stock is a type of preferred share that pays a dividend and can be converted into common stock at a fixed conversion ratio after a specified time.
