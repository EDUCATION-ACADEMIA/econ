RISK AND RETURN

[SOURCES]
If the value of the expected return is higher than the required returns, then the stock:

(A) ** Undervalued

(B) Fairly valued

(C) Overvalued

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -If the rate of return is greater than the expected return, it would be considered an overvalued security. If the rate of return is less than expected returns, it would be regarded as undervalued security.

Concept note-2: -If the expected return of an investment does not meet or exceed the required rate of return, the investor will not invest. The required rate of return is also called the hurdle rate of return.

Concept note-3: -An undervalued stock is defined as a stock that is selling at a price significantly below what is assumed to be its intrinsic value. For example, if a stock is selling for $50, but it is worth $100 based on predictable future cash flows, then it is an undervalued stock.

Concept note-4: -To calculate it, divide the market price per share by the book value per share. A stock could be undervalued if the P/B ratio is lower than 1. P/B ratio example: ABC’s shares are selling for $50 a share, and its book value is $70, which means the P/B ratio is 0.71 ($50/$70).

Concept note-5: -The sales per share metric is calculated by dividing a company’s 12-month sales by the number of outstanding shares. A low P/S ratio in comparison to peers could suggest some undervaluation. A high P/S ratio would suggest overvaluation.
